The defining feature of most software shipped in the last decade isn't what
it does — it's how loudly it asks to be reopened. Streaks, badges, weekly
digests, push notifications about push notifications, the whole grammar of
the attention economy stitched into things that used to be tools.

We're trying to build the other thing. Software that adds something to your
day and then steps back. No nudges that exist for engagement metrics. No
asking for permission to send notifications about something you didn't sign
up for. No dark patterns dressed up as features. The highest praise a tool
we built can get is *I forgot it was running — it just worked*.

This isn't a moral position so much as a taste one. Quiet software is
harder to ship — it has fewer hooks, fewer charts go up and to the right,
no growth team to lean on. But it ages better. People come back to it on
their own, because they remembered it was good, not because a notification
yanked them back in.
